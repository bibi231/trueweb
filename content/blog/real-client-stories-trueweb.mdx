The best argument for investing in your web presence is not a sales pitch — it's someone else's result. Here are three stories from Nigerian businesses that made the investment and what happened after.

## Case Study 1: Lagos Fashion Retailer

**The before state:**
A Lagos-based fashion brand had been running on a Wix site for 3 years. The site looked decent on desktop but was painful on mobile. Checkout required multiple steps, customers kept abandoning at the payment page, and the owner was taking orders manually via WhatsApp DMs and writing them in a notebook.

Monthly online orders: ~12. Most revenue still came from walk-in traffic at her Lekki store.

**What we changed:**
- Migrated to a Next.js storefront with Zustand cart state and Squad checkout
- Simplified checkout to 3 steps: cart → contact details → payment
- Added product filtering by size, color, and category
- Integrated WhatsApp order notifications (owner gets a WhatsApp message for each order — familiar, no dashboard needed initially)
- Image optimization: product photos from 2–4MB each to under 200KB each using WebP + CDN
- Mobile page load time: 14 seconds → 2.1 seconds

**The results (3 months post-launch):**
- Monthly online orders: 12 → 67
- Cart abandonment rate dropped 41%
- WhatsApp DM orders reduced (customers now complete purchase on site instead)
- She expanded inventory because she could see what sold and what didn't

The owner told us: "Before, I didn't trust my website to take money. Now it sells while I sleep."

## Case Study 2: Abuja Consulting Firm

**The before state:**
A management consulting practice with 8 staff and clients across 3 sectors. They had no website — leads came entirely through referral and LinkedIn. The founder wanted a web presence that matched the quality of their client engagements.

The problem: every web agency they approached quoted ₦800,000–₦1.5M for something that looked "enterprise". They didn't need enterprise — they needed credibility.

**What we changed:**
- Built a focused 5-page site: Home, Services, Case Studies (3 detailed examples), Team, Contact
- Added a "Request a Proposal" form wired to their team email via Resend
- Wrote case study copy with them (the hardest part — getting consultants to share enough detail to be convincing)
- Set up Google Business Profile for Abuja location
- Added JSON-LD structured data for Organization schema
- Connected Analytics with goal tracking on form submissions

Total cost: Under ₦300,000. Launched in 3 weeks.

**The results (6 months post-launch):**
- 3 inbound leads directly attributable to website (Google search and LinkedIn clicks to site)
- One of those 3 converted to a ₦4.2M engagement
- The founder now sends the site URL to prospects before first meetings — changed how meetings start

The partner told us: "It doesn't feel like a small firm's website anymore. That matters in our industry."

## Case Study 3: Port Harcourt Logistics Company

**The before state:**
A last-mile delivery business serving 4 states. They had a Facebook page and a phone number. Business was growing (40 drivers, 200+ deliveries/week) but everything was tracked on WhatsApp groups and spreadsheets. Customers had no tracking visibility.

They came to us asking for "a tracking system." What they actually needed was a customer-facing status page and a basic admin dashboard — not a full logistics platform.

**What we changed:**
- Customer portal: enter waybill number → see status (Picked up / In transit / Delivered)
- Admin dashboard: update delivery status per waybill, bulk update via CSV, see pending deliveries
- Automated SMS to customers on status change (via Termii, a Nigerian SMS API)
- Basic invoice generation for their corporate clients
- Mobile-first design — all drivers and customers primarily on mobile

We explicitly did not build: route optimization, automated dispatch, driver tracking. Those are phase 2 problems. Phase 1 is reducing the "where is my package?" WhatsApp messages the founder was answering personally.

**The results (4 months post-launch):**
- "Where is my package" messages reduced by ~70% (estimate from founder)
- Corporate client renewals improved — they could now offer tracking as a differentiator
- Admin time on status updates: ~45 min/day → ~10 min/day (dashboard vs. WhatsApp messages)
- One corporate client increased their monthly volume because they trusted the tracking

The founder told us: "My customers now feel like they're dealing with a real logistics company, not a guy with a WhatsApp group."

## The common thread

None of these transformations required enterprise-grade engineering. What they required was:

1. **Understanding the real problem** — not the surface request
2. **Solving it completely** — payment flows that actually complete, forms that actually deliver, tracking that actually works
3. **Prioritizing mobile** — every one of these businesses has a predominantly mobile user base
4. **Measuring outcomes** — knowing whether the investment worked

The web presence gap between Nigerian SMEs and what their clients expect is closing fast. The businesses investing in getting there first are capturing the leads that others are leaving behind.
