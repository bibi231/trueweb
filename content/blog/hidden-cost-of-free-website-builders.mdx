Free website builders are genuinely useful in some situations. If you need a personal portfolio and you're not a developer, Wix does the job. But for Nigerian businesses making real money from their web presence, "free" platforms carry hidden costs that most founders discover too late.

## What "free" actually means

Free website builders make money through:
1. **Your data** — they own your traffic data and can use it
2. **Plan upgrades** — you'll hit the free tier limits quickly
3. **Hostage hosting** — your content is trapped in their format
4. **Advertising** — free plans usually display platform ads on your site
5. **Transaction fees** — free e-commerce plans charge a % of every sale

Let's go through the actual costs for Nigerian businesses specifically.

## Cost 1: Performance penalty (→ lost revenue)

Free and low-cost tiers on Wix, Weebly, and Shopify Lite use shared infrastructure. Every site on the tier competes for the same server resources.

**The numbers:** An average Wix site on a free/starter plan loads in 4–8 seconds on mobile in Nigeria. A Next.js site on Vercel loads in 1–2 seconds.

**What this means in money:** If your site gets 1,000 monthly visitors and loads in 5 seconds instead of 2 seconds, you're losing approximately 30–40% of those visitors before they see your content (industry data on 3–5 second abandonment rates). At a conservative ₦5,000 average order value and 2% conversion rate, that's 12–16 lost orders per month. At ₦5,000 each, that's ₦60,000–₦80,000/month in invisible lost revenue.

The "free" platform costs you more than a proper hosting plan would.

## Cost 2: No custom domain (or expensive one)

Free plans on most website builders give you a subdomain like `yourbusiness.wixsite.com/yourbrand`. This looks unprofessional. For Nigerian businesses especially, the `.com.ng` domain signals local legitimacy that `wixsite.com` cannot.

Wix's cheapest plan that includes a custom domain costs $16/month (roughly ₦25,000/month at current rates). A `.com.ng` domain + Vercel hosting costs approximately ₦4,000/year + ₦0/month.

## Cost 3: Platform transaction fees

Most "free" e-commerce builders charge a percentage of every sale, on top of payment gateway fees.

| Platform | Transaction fee (free/basic plan) |
|---|---|
| Shopify Starter | 5% per transaction |
| Wix eCommerce (free) | No e-commerce on free plan |
| Squarespace Personal | No e-commerce |
| Squarespace Business | 3% per transaction |
| Custom + Squad | 0% platform fee, 1.5% + ₦100 gateway fee |

On ₦1,000,000/month in sales, Shopify Starter's 5% transaction fee = ₦50,000/month in platform fees alone. A custom solution adds zero platform fees on top of the gateway fees you'd pay anyway.

## Cost 4: No payment localization

Nigerian businesses need:
- Naira pricing
- Bank transfer as a payment method (many customers prefer it)
- USSD options
- Local card support (Verve cards especially)

Shopify's Nigerian support for local payment methods is limited. Wix's payment integrations in Nigeria don't include Squad or direct Nigerian bank transfer. You end up with a payment flow your customers abandon.

This is not a minor inconvenience — it's the difference between a customer completing a purchase and going to your competitor's WhatsApp instead.

## Cost 5: Migration difficulty

This is the cost nobody warns you about until you've lived it.

After 2–3 years on Wix, your site has:
- Hundreds of Wix blog posts in Wix's proprietary format
- Product listings in Wix's database
- Customer data in Wix's CRM
- Email lists in Wix's email marketing tool

None of this exports cleanly to a standard format. Migrating from Wix to a custom build is a forensic exercise, not a technical one. You're extracting content from a proprietary system and reformatting it into something useful.

We've done these migrations. They cost ₦150,000–₦400,000 in labor and take 4–8 weeks. That's a hidden cost you pay at year 3 because "free" seemed better at year 1.

## Cost 6: SEO ceiling

Wix SEO has improved significantly, but free-tier platforms have structural SEO limitations:
- Shared IP addresses (you share an IP with thousands of other sites, some of which may be spammy)
- No `<link rel="canonical">` control in many themes
- Core Web Vitals scores typically lower due to platform JavaScript overhead
- No control over server-side rendering or caching headers

Google uses Core Web Vitals as a ranking signal. Platforms with structurally worse performance scores have structurally lower SEO ceilings, regardless of content quality.

## When free website builders are the right choice

In fairness: free builders are the right choice when:
- You're testing a business idea and don't want to invest until the concept validates
- You're a non-technical solo operator who needs something live this weekend
- The site is informational only (no payments, no lead capture that needs reliability)
- You have < 6 months runway and every naira matters

In these cases, use the free builder, validate your model, and migrate when you're ready.

## The right question

Not: "What's the cheapest way to get online?"

But: "What does my web presence need to do to grow my business, and what's the cheapest way to build something that does that?"

For most Nigerian businesses, the answer involves Squad payments, local domains, mobile-optimized performance, and reliability. None of those come free.
