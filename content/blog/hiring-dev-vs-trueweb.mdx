When your business needs a website or software, you have three paths: hire a freelancer, engage an agency, or use a product like TrueWeb. Each makes sense in different situations. This guide breaks down the tradeoffs honestly — including when TrueWeb is *not* the right answer.

## Option 1: Hire a Nigerian freelancer

**When it works well:**
- You have a technical co-founder or in-house developer who can manage them
- The project is small and well-defined (landing page, one integration)
- You have time to review work and iterate
- Budget is under ₦200,000

**The real risks:**
- Dropout rate is high: many Nigerian freelancers juggle 5+ clients simultaneously
- Ownership of code is ambiguous unless you have a clear contract
- Maintenance after project ends is usually a separate negotiation
- Quality varies dramatically — there is no credential system, only referrals
- Timeline slippage is common; "ready in 2 weeks" often means 6–8

**What to check before hiring:**
1. Live URLs of previous work (not screenshots)
2. Can they explain what tech stack they used and why?
3. Do they have a GitHub profile with activity?
4. What happens if something breaks after handoff?

Freelancers who can answer all four confidently are the good ones. They're rare and usually booked.

## Option 2: Hire a Nigerian web agency

**When it works well:**
- Project is complex (custom e-commerce, internal tools, mobile app)
- You need multiple skills: design, backend, frontend, DevOps
- Budget is ₦500,000+
- You need SLAs, contracts, and invoices for compliance

**The real risks:**
- Most "agencies" in Nigeria are 2–3 person operations that outsource work
- Account manager promises vs. developer capacity often diverge
- Handoff quality varies: some agencies retain ownership of code or credentials
- Ongoing maintenance can become expensive lock-in
- Mid-project turnover — a key developer leaves — can stall projects indefinitely

**What to check before engaging:**
1. Ask to speak directly with the developer, not just the account manager
2. Request a contract specifying who owns the code and credentials at delivery
3. Ask about their deployment infrastructure — do they control your hosting?
4. Get referrals from clients in your industry specifically

- Budget is defined and you want predictable pricing

## The question that matters most

Not "which option is cheapest?" but "which option lets me focus on my actual business?"

For a business owner who's good at running a restaurant, e-commerce store, or consultancy — and not at managing software projects — the most expensive thing is time spent on development problems.

A freelancer at ₦150,000 who takes 4 months and requires weekly check-ins is not cheaper than a system that costs more but runs itself.
